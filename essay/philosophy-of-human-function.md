# *The Philosophy of Human Function and Judgment: From Understanding State to the Decision Loop*

This is an **original framework for understanding the human body proposed by Movetips**.  
It is not a simple relabeling of existing medical, rehabilitation, or training language.  
It is not a repackaging of old ideas under new terms.  
What it tries to do is bring the long-fragmented understanding of the body across medicine, rehabilitation, training, manual therapy, and performance back to a deeper and more unified starting point.

That starting point is:

**Human function.**

What I mean by “human function” is not a diagnosis, not the name of a movement, and not a particular method, technique, or treatment approach.  
It is not a score, a table, or a local conclusion.

**Human function is the body’s ability to be normally called upon.**

This sentence looks simple, but what it really changes is the way we look at the body.

For a long time, when people talked about the body, they usually started with pain, tightness, weakness, injury, disease, or movement faults.  
All of that matters.  
But none of it is the deepest layer of the problem.

The real underlying question is:

**Can this person’s body still be normally called upon?**

Because the body is not something that exists merely to be analyzed.  
The body exists first to live life.

A person must stand, walk, sit, sleep, work, study, care for family, recover, exercise, and respond to the real demands of everyday life.  
So the most fundamental meaning of the body is not “what it has,” but “whether it can be organized and mobilized in real life.”

That is why I use the word **calling** to rethink human function.

It is a plain word, but it pulls many scattered problems back into one line of understanding.

**Who is calling on the body?**  
Not in the abstract.  
Tasks call on the body.  
Contexts call on the body.  
The demands of life call on the body.  
A person’s problem does not appear out of nowhere. It appears while standing, walking, lifting, training, recovering, working, and living.

**What is being called upon?**  
Not just one local part.  
What is being called upon is structure, energy, and control.  
In plain terms, the body must first have the basic conditions needed to do what life asks of it.  
This means we should not look only at one local area or only at the visible movement. We should look at what the body is actually relying on to support the task.

**How is the body being called upon?**  
Not by adding isolated parts together.  
It works through coordination.  
The body is not a pile of parts. It is a coordinated whole.  
Who leads, who supports, where force is transferred, where compensation appears, where the system breaks down—these determine whether the body can do the task smoothly.

**What happens when calling fails?**  
Most often, there are three basic situations: there is not enough, it cannot be organized, or it cannot be done.  
“Not enough” means the conditions are insufficient.  
“Cannot be organized” means there may be some capacity, but it cannot be put together properly.  
“Cannot be done” means the current situation has already exceeded the body’s boundary.  
Many people rush to conclusions too quickly. But if they first clarify these three forms of failure, they can avoid many unnecessary mistakes.

**What if the cost of calling is too high?**  
Then compensation, risk, and unsustainability begin to appear.  
In other words, some people are not unable to do the task—they are doing it at an increasingly high cost.  
They are not incapable—they are forcing their way through it.  
They are not failing to complete the task—but the price of completion is too high.  
When that happens, the body is no longer functioning in a normal way, even if the task is still being completed on the surface.

**How do we restore normal calling?**  
That is where assessment, intervention, and training come in.  
Assessment is not for filling up forms.  
Intervention is not for showing off techniques.  
Training is not for making movement look busy.  
Their real purpose is to help the body return to a state where it can be called upon more smoothly, more stably, and at a lower cost.

So the word **calling** is not decorative language.  
It actually holds up the entire logic of how we understand the body.

For years, many fields have talked about the body.  
Some start from disease.  
Some start from pain.  
Some start from movement.  
Some start from manual methods.  
Some start from training.  
None of these approaches is wrong.  
But the problem is that many people are dealing with the same human body without starting from the same underlying point.

The result is that knowledge keeps growing, methods keep growing, terminology keeps growing, but the deeper language that can bring everything back to the question of “what is happening to this person right now” has remained unstable.

This is exactly why I am writing **The Philosophy of Human Function and Judgment: From Understanding State to the Decision Loop**.

What matters most about this book is not that it invents another term.  
What matters is that it tries to reorganize many things that people have long understood through habit, intuition, and industry tradition into a clearer path.

It is not trying to solve abstract problems in the air.  
It is trying to solve real ones:

Why can the same person be described so differently by different professions?  
Why do many people work hard and still become more confused?  
Why do some practitioners know how to do things but cannot clearly explain what they are actually solving?  
Why do so many services fail to be carried forward, repeated, or handed off clearly?

The real strength of this book is that it tries to make these things clear.

It is not trying to turn doctors into coaches.  
It is not trying to turn coaches into therapists.  
It is not trying to make massage practitioners replace anyone else.  
What it is trying to do is something deeper:

**To help different professionals return to the same object when facing the same human body.**

Once that happens, the value becomes very direct.

For doctors, it helps connect “disease” more closely to “the person’s real functional state in life.”  
It helps them go beyond the diagnosis itself and further ask: what can this person actually do right now, and where are the real boundaries?

For therapists, it helps turn what is often clear in the mind but difficult to express into something easier to communicate.  
It helps answer: how is the body being called upon right now, where has the pattern become disordered, why is the cost high, and what should be adjusted next?

For trainers and coaches, it helps move training beyond just watching visible movement.  
It is no longer only about whether a movement “looks right,” but whether the person is functioning normally, compensating, detouring, or burning through their capacity too early.  
In this way, training becomes not only about leading exercise, but about making clearer judgments.

For massage practitioners, this book also has real value.  
Many massage practitioners work with bodily problems every day, but much of the language available to them remains at the level of “what feels tight,” “what feels uncomfortable,” or “what feels off.”  
This book offers a deeper path of understanding:  
not stopping at sensation, but going one step further to ask how this person’s body is actually being called upon, why it breaks down under use, why it tires so easily, and why the same issues keep returning.

Once someone can see at that level, their service becomes more stable, more coherent, and more convincing.

So the value of this book is not just “learning more theory.”  
Its real force is that it helps people raise their professional ability by one whole level.

Not only doing, but seeing more clearly.  
Not only speaking about parts, but speaking about the whole.  
Not only handling problems by habit, but explaining them clearly, handing them over clearly, and carrying them forward clearly.

To put it more directly:

In the future, what will really matter is not only whether someone can perform a few techniques or deliver a few services.  
What will matter more is **whether they can see bodily problems clearly, explain them clearly, and handle them clearly**.

Whoever masters that ability earlier will be in a much stronger position.

Because once you can truly explain human function clearly, what others feel is no longer only “you know how to do something.”  
They begin to feel that “you really understand what problem you are solving.”  
That directly affects trust, delivery, reputation, and income.

Why?

Because in many professions, the real competition is not just about whether you can do something with your hands.  
It is about whether you can see a complex problem clearly, explain it in a way people understand, and handle it in a way that makes people feel safe.  
Once you can do that, what you provide is no longer just a service.  
You provide a kind of judgment that is much harder to replace.

And that is exactly what **The Philosophy of Human Function and Judgment: From Understanding State to the Decision Loop** is trying to do.

It is not an ordinary book of knowledge.  
It is closer to rebuilding an underlying framework:  
so that the body is no longer treated as only disease, only movement, or only technique,  
but is returned to the real starting point—how a person lives, and how the body is called upon in life.

This book is worth paying attention to not because its title sounds big,  
but because it is trying to address a very old problem that many professions have never truly solved:

**When facing the same person, where should we begin, how should we make sense of the problem, and how can we make the next steps more stable?**

I believe that once this is made clear, it will help far more than one profession.  
It will help doctors connect more clearly with the people who come after them.  
It will help therapists express their value more clearly.  
It will help trainers move from simply leading exercise to making stronger judgments.  
And it will help massage practitioners move from intuition alone toward a more stable way of understanding and explaining the body.

That is why I would say this book is not just another book.  
It is trying to rebuild a deeper way of seeing the human body.
